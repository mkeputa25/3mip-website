# 3MIP — Independent Project Analysis

This is an independent analysis of 3MIP — the Mobility and Migration Modeling Intercomparison Project — based on the current Weebly site, public sources, and adjacent project documentation. It exists to give Claude Code context about *what 3MIP actually is* and *what the current website does well and badly*, separate from the project brief.

This is the "what we're working with" document. The brief tells you what to build; this tells you why.

## TL;DR

- **3MIP is the Mobility and Migration Modeling Intercomparison Project** — a credible, freshly-launched (October 2025) academic hackathon co-led by Andrew Bell (Cornell), Kelsea Best (Ohio State), and Lars Tierolf (VU Amsterdam) that asks climate-migration modelers to apply their diverse model architectures to a single benchmark case (coastal Bangladesh) using a shared, curated input dataset, with the goal of producing a *Climatic Change* topical collection and an iEMSs 2026 Dublin synthesis session.
- **The substance is real and the people are serious; the packaging is amateur.** The Weebly site is shoestring — free template, no logo branding consistency, broken/under-construction forms, a typo in the timeline, two webinar recordings hosted as raw Google Drive embeds — but the principal investigators, advisory team, and intellectual lineage are first-tier in the climate-mobility modeling field.
- **The honest verdict: not abandoned, not derivative, not yet validated.** As of May 2026, 3MIP has produced no peer-reviewed outputs, no preprints, no public list of registered participating teams, and no dedicated grant has been publicly identified — but it is just ~7 months into a recruitment phase whose deliverables (iEMSs 2026 panel, *Climatic Change* submissions late 2026 onward) are still ahead. Judge it again after the July 2026 Dublin session.

## Key findings

**Full name and identity.** 3MIP stands for the Mobility and Migration Modeling Intercomparison Project (sometimes rendered as "Model Intercomparison" on the home page and "Modeling Intercomparison" in the iEMSs program — a minor internal inconsistency to fix in v1). It is explicitly modeled on, and names as inspiration, **AgMIP** and **ISIMIP** — both of which are major, multi-institutional, NSF/DFG/EU-funded enterprises with hundreds of papers and IPCC contributions.

**Origin and funding lineage.** 3MIP grew directly out of the Climate Migration Modeling Intercomparison Workshop held at Princeton on September 25–27, 2024, organized by Choquette-Levy and Cottier and convening more than 30 researchers from three continents. That workshop was funded by the LDEO Climate Center (Columbia), the Carolee Bol and Scott Rosenberg '95 C-PREE Fund at Princeton, Cornell's Department of Global Development, and the UN Global Centre for Climate Mobility. The workshop's stated next step was to "develop a proposal for an NSF Research Coordination Network or similar opportunity to fund long-term (e.g. 3+ years) climate migration model intercomparison research."

**Important sourcing caveat: no public record of a dedicated NSF, Belmont Forum, ERC, or philanthropic grant explicitly awarded to 3MIP for its 2025–2027 operating phase.** The site names only institutional sponsors. Whether the planned NSF RCN application has been submitted, funded, or is still pending is not disclosed. The website should not name operating-phase funders without explicit Bell sign-off.

**Substantive focus.** The first 3MIP "hackathon" benchmark is coastal mobility in Bangladesh — chosen for empirical density, abundant data, and policy relevance in a country of 175.7 million people. Modelers register, agree to a common curated input dataset, but are free to choose their own research questions, model architectures (ABM, gravity, radiation, IAM, ML), and output variables. The intercomparison is **not** a controlled "same question, same outputs" exercise like CMIP6 — it is closer to a "shared sandbox" approach. This is deliberate (lower friction to participation) but methodologically Phase 1.

## What the current Weebly site does well

There is exactly one thing worth keeping: **the Data page.** It provides a clean, linked, citable table of input datasets with DOI references and original paper citations. Port this content. Upgrade the typography. Keep the structure.

Everything else needs replacement.

## What the current Weebly site does badly

**Platform and design.** Free Weebly subdomain (3mip.weebly.com), Weebly upsell footer ("Powered by Create your own unique website with customizable templates") on every page. No custom domain. No professional branding system. Logo at inconsistent sizes across pages. For a project run by tenured Cornell, OSU, and VU faculty, this is below the floor for an institutional research initiative.

**Information architecture.** Seven shallow pages with no News section, no Outputs section, no place for publications, and webinar recordings buried at the bottom of the Modelers & Experts page with no context. The IA has no room for what the project is about to produce.

**Clarity of mission.** The home page does state what 3MIP is, which is a strength. The Context and Problem Statement page is reasonably written. These are the second and third things worth preserving conceptually, even if the copy needs rewriting.

**Usability and mobile.** Renders acceptably on mobile (Weebly defaults are responsive) but lacks any privacy notice, GDPR statement, or accessibility commitment. The Participate form is functional but minimal.

**Depth vs. surface.** Zero PDFs, zero preprints, zero white papers, zero working papers, zero policy briefs to download. Two webinar videos as raw Google Drive embeds, no abstracts, no slide decks, no transcripts.

**Quality flags.** Typo in the timeline ("Early November, 2026" sandwiched between October 2025 and December 2025 events). Acronym rendered inconsistently between the home page and the iEMSs program. Contact email obfuscated via Cloudflare email protection — works but awkward.

**Bottom line:** Weebly suggests low budget, and in this case the packaging *under-sells* the substance. A professional landing page at a real domain would dramatically improve credibility for no extra intellectual effort.

## Landscape positioning

3MIP sits in a crowded but uncoordinated subfield.

- **AgMIP** and **ISIMIP** — the explicit templates. Far larger (hundreds of modelers, multi-million-dollar funding, decade-plus runtime, regular IPCC contributions) but operate in established quantitative domains where output variables are well-defined.
- **CIESIN/Columbia Climate Mobility Network** and the **UN GCCM Knowledge & Policy Hub** — overlapping but more policy-oriented than modeling-focused. Alex de Sherbinin straddles both 3MIP (advisor) and these.
- **COASTMOVE** (VU Amsterdam, ERC, 2021–2026) — global coastal-migration ABM, Tierolf's home project. Complementary, not competing.
- **World Bank Groundswell reports** — top-down country-level migration projections; 3MIP would feed bottom-up alternatives.
- **HABITABLE** (EU Horizon 2020), **MISTY**, **PRISE** — adjacent climate-migration consortia, more policy/empirics focused.

**Distinctive angle.** 3MIP is, as far as can be verified, the first explicit migration-model intercomparison effort modeled on the AgMIP/ISIMIP playbook. That novelty is its main asset. Whether the AgMIP/ISIMIP template can survive the conceptual heterogeneity of migration modeling — where "migration" itself is operationalized differently across ABMs, gravity models, and IAMs — is an open empirical question that the project will answer one way or another.

## Implications for the website build

What this analysis means for design and content decisions:

1. **The credibility bar is real and the substance can clear it.** Build at the level of agmip.org, isimip.org, cpree.princeton.edu. The substance is there to back the visual register.

2. **Outputs scaffolding is necessary before content exists.** The site must have a place for working papers, the *Climatic Change* collection, and benchmark results before any of those exist. Empty states are honest if labeled honestly.

3. **The "what is 3MIP" pitch must work without funder names.** No publicly identifiable operating-phase grant exists. The credibility comes from the team and the AgMIP/ISIMIP lineage. Lean on those.

4. **The methodological honesty is a feature.** 3MIP's "shared sandbox" approach is methodologically Phase 1, not CMIP6-strict. The site should say so. Owning the limitation is more credible than pretending it isn't there.

5. **The signature visualization is the whole pitch.** A small multiples chart showing how different model archetypes project the same Bangladesh migration question differently is the entire reason 3MIP exists. It belongs on the home page, above the fold, and should be the first thing a visitor sees after the headline.

6. **Bangladesh as a case is well-defended by precedent.** Bell et al. 2021 *Environmental Research Letters* is the empirical motivation. The Data page provides the methodological backbone. The Case 1 page should connect those two.

7. **The handoff plan is structurally important.** Mario leaves in June 2026. Content updates after that (new news items, papers, webinars, team members) must be addable by a non-developer via Markdown. The MAINTAINERS.md document is not optional polish; it is project-survival infrastructure.

## Verdict (repeated for emphasis)

3MIP is a real, substantive, well-led, under-packaged, and not-yet-proven academic infrastructure project. It is neither abandoned nor derivative; it is freshly launched, staffed by serious people from the right institutions, and pursuing a genuinely useful intellectual goal with a credible first case. The Weebly website is the weakest element. The substance exceeds the packaging, but only narrowly, because the substance has not yet produced peer-reviewable outputs.

The website's job is to make the substance legible at the credibility level of the people behind it, in time for the July 2026 iEMSs Dublin session.
